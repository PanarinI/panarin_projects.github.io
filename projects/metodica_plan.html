Reference materials

Target language
robbery
Meaning: stealing money/property from a person or bank, often by force
Form: noun, C /ˈrɒbəri/
CCQs: «Is violence needed? (often yes)»; «Is it taking or destroying? (taking)»
burglary
Meaning: illegally entering a building and stealing from it
Form: noun, C /ˈbɜːɡləri/
CCQs: «Do you break in? (yes)»; «Do you use force on people? (not necessarily)»
mugging
Meaning: attacking someone in a public place and stealing from them
Form: noun, C /ˈmʌɡɪŋ/
CCQs: «Is this in a public place? (yes)»; «Do you take by force? (yes)»
shoplifting
Meaning: stealing goods from a shop without paying
Form: noun, U /ˈʃɒplɪftɪŋ/
CCQs: «Do you steal money? (no, goods)»; «Is this in a shop? (yes)»
vandalism
Meaning: intentionally damaging public or private property
Form: noun, U /ˈvændəlɪzəm/
CCQs: «Is property damaged? (yes)»; «Is it theft? (no)»
arson
Meaning: deliberately setting fire to property
Form: noun, U /ˈɑːsən/
CCQs: «Do you burn? (yes)»; «Is it accidental? (no)»
smuggling
Meaning: illegally bringing goods into or out of a country
Form: noun, U /ˈsmʌɡlɪŋ/
CCQs: «Is this across borders? (yes)»; «Do you pay customs? (no)»
kidnapping
Meaning: taking someone away illegally and asking for ransom
Form: noun, C /ˈkɪdnæpɪŋ/
CCQs: «Is there a person taken? (yes)»; «Is it legal? (no)»
murder
Meaning: unlawful killing of another person
Form: noun, C /ˈmɜːdər/
CCQs: «Is someone killed? (yes)»; «Is it an accident? (no)»
cybercrime
Meaning: illegal activities committed on the Internet/computers
Form: noun, U /ˈsaɪbərˌkraɪm/
CCQs: «Is this on the Internet? (yes)»; «Is it offline? (no)»

C. Detective verbs (фразы)
search for evidence /sɜːrtʃ/
Meaning: look carefully for clues/proofs
Form: verb + prep
CCQs: «Do you look for clues? (yes)»; «Is it at the end of an investigation? (no)»
analyse evidence /ˈænəlaɪz/
Meaning: study evidence in detail
CCQs: «Do you look in detail? (yes)»; «Is analyse = ignore? (no)»
question a witness /ˈkwes.tʃən/
Meaning: ask someone who saw the crime what happened
CCQs: «Did they see? (yes)»; «Are they forced? (no)»
accuse a suspect /əˈkjuːz/
Meaning: say someone did the crime (without proof yet)
CCQs: «Is it proof? (not yet)»; «Do you have evidence? (maybe)»
charge a suspect /tʃɑːrdʒ/
Meaning: formally state in court the crime they did
CCQs: «Is it official? (yes)»; «Is charge = accuse? (stronger)»
arrest a suspect /əˈrɛst/
Meaning: take them to a police station lawfully
CCQs: «Do they go to prison? (no)»; «Is there force? (sometimes)»
prove something /pruːv/
Meaning: show with evidence that it is true
CCQs: «Do you need evidence? (yes)»; «Is prove = guess? (no)»
investigate a case /ɪnˈvɛstɪɡeɪt/
Meaning: try to find out what happened and who did it
CCQs: «Is this the first step? (yes)»; «Is it done after arrest? (no)»

2. Методические советы
  • Всегда проверяйте понимание не общей фразой «understand?», а с помощью CCQ: «Does ‘mugging’ always involve a weapon?».
  • Для подростков актуальны конкурсы: можно в practice добавить quick‑race за правильные gap‑fills.
  • На этапе Production стимулируйте использование всех новых слов: выдавайте бонусные баллы за каждое использованное целевое слово.
